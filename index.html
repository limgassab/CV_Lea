Research profile

I am an interdisciplinary physicist working at the interface of quantum biology, open quantum systems, quantum information, molecular biophysics, computational modeling, and experimental biophysics. My current research examines quantum-biological processes in neuronal microtubules and their possible relevance to brain function and neurodegenerative disease, with emphasis on excitation dynamics, light-matter interactions, aromatic amino-acid networks, and oxidative modification.

My doctoral training was primarily theoretical. During my postdoctoral work, I expanded into spectroscopy, imaging, biochemical methods, and mass spectrometry. This combination allows me to develop mechanistic models, translate them into experimentally testable predictions, and collaborate across physics, biology, computation, and engineering.

Research themes

My work spans several connected areas. The themes below provide context, while the publication list remains complete and chronological.

Microtubule quantum biology and photonics

Excitation transport, information flow, cooperative optical effects, disorder, and tunable ultraviolet responses in tubulin and microtubule aromatic networks.

Microtubules Excitons Photonics Spectroscopy

Oxidative damage and experimental biophysics

Experimental workflows for tubulin polymerization, optical emission, microscopy, biochemical perturbation, and peptide-resolved analysis of oxidative modification.

Oxidative stress Mass spectrometry Imaging Biochemistry

Quantum sensing, metrology, and coherence

Quantum-enhanced measurement, nitrogen-vacancy sensing, polarimetric estimation, and geometrical strategies for preserving quantum resources in noisy systems.

Quantum sensing Metrology NV centers Decoherence

Quantum information in biology, cognition, and memory

Quantum-information approaches to biological systems, psychophysical detection, models of cognition, and controlled benchmarks for context-sensitive associative memory.

Quantum cognition Vision Memory Plasticity
